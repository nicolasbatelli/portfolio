3D design and printing became a creative outlet for me alongside software development. Here's how my setup and workflow evolved over the years.

## Where I started

My first steps were modeling in **AutoCAD** and printing on an **Anet A8**. The A8 is a bare-bones, build-it-yourself kit, so I learned a lot the hard way: bed leveling, adhesion, temperature tuning, and all the little quirks of FDM printing.

## Leveling up

Eventually I bought an **Ender 3 V2** and switched my design work to **Autodesk Inventor**. Inventor's parametric, assembly-driven workflow was a huge upgrade for designing functional, mechanical parts — and it's where I've created most of my models since.

## My setup today

These days I run a small print farm:

- **2x Ender 3 V2** — reliable workhorses for everyday prints
- **1x Bambu Lab A1 Combo** — fast, multi-color printing with AMS

## Design for printing

A few things I keep in mind when designing:

- Avoid steep overhangs without support
- Consider print orientation early
- Add fillets to reduce stress concentrations
- Design tolerances around real-world fit, especially for mechanical parts

## What I've built

I've designed a lot of parts in Inventor — from household items to functional automotive and RC components. You can explore some of them on the [3D Models](/models) page, where you can rotate each part in 3D and download the STLs.
